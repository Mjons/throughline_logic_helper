# Pitch & Sales Frameworks

Narrative structures designed for persuasion — investor decks, enterprise sales calls, cold outreach, and demo walkthroughs. Each framework below is described as a beat sequence with example options, ready to be converted into a Throughline template.

---

## 1. Sequoia Pitch Deck

The industry-standard VC pitch structure. Every beat maps to a slide. Designed for 10–15 minute presentations to institutional investors.

**Best for:** Seed through Series B fundraising decks

### Beats

#### Beat 1: Problem

_What pain exists in the world that's worth solving?_

| Option                                 | Description                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| -------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Quantified market pain**             | X million people spend Y hours/dollars doing Z badly. Lead with the number, then the human cost. Works when the problem is measurable and large.                                                     |
| **Narrative pain — a day in the life** | Walk through one person's actual experience with the broken status quo. Name them. Show the friction. Works when the problem is visceral but hard to quantify.                                       |
| **Expert consensus pain**              | Industry analysts, research papers, or regulatory bodies have named this problem. The audience trusts authority — borrow it. Works when the problem is emerging and the audience hasn't felt it yet. |
| **Competitive landscape pain**         | Existing solutions are bad and everyone knows it. NPS scores are low, churn is high, the incumbents haven't innovated in a decade. The problem isn't unsolved — it's solved badly.                   |

#### Beat 2: Solution

_What do you do about it?_

| Option                          | Description                                                                                                                                                             |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **One-sentence product thesis** | "We are X for Y" or "We do X so that Y can Z." Maximum clarity, minimum jargon. Works when the product is genuinely simple.                                             |
| **Before/after transformation** | Show the user's world before your product, then after. The delta is your value. Works when the transformation is dramatic and visual.                                   |
| **Architecture insight**        | The reason existing solutions fail is a structural assumption you reject. Name the wrong assumption, then name yours. Works for deep-tech or infrastructure plays.      |
| **Demo-first**                  | Skip the abstraction. Show the product doing the thing. 30 seconds of screen recording replaces three slides of explanation. Works when the product is polished enough. |

#### Beat 3: Why Now

_What changed that makes this possible or necessary today?_

| Option                   | Description                                                                                                                                   |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Technology unlock**    | A new capability (AI, blockchain, API, hardware) just crossed a threshold that makes your approach feasible for the first time.               |
| **Regulatory shift**     | New regulation creates demand (compliance) or removes barriers (deregulation). The timing isn't your choice — it's the market's.              |
| **Behavioral shift**     | Users changed. Remote work, mobile-first, Gen Z expectations, post-pandemic habits. The demand was always there; the behavior just caught up. |
| **Market failure event** | An incumbent collapsed, a major product shut down, or a high-profile breach eroded trust. The vacuum is your opening.                         |

#### Beat 4: Market Size

_How big is the opportunity?_

| Option                         | Description                                                                                                                                 |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **TAM/SAM/SOM waterfall**      | Total addressable → serviceable addressable → serviceable obtainable. The classic. Works when you have credible third-party sizing.         |
| **Bottom-up unit economics**   | X potential customers × Y average contract value = Z market. More credible than top-down for niche or novel markets.                        |
| **Wedge-and-expand**           | Start in a $500M niche you can dominate, then show the adjacent markets that open once you own the wedge. Works for platform plays.         |
| **Comparable company analogy** | "Company X built a $B business doing Y for Z. We're doing Y' for Z' where the market is N× larger." Works when a relevant precedent exists. |

#### Beat 5: Product

_What does it actually do? Show it._

| Option                               | Description                                                                                                                                                    |
| ------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Live demo**                        | The product running in real time. Highest risk, highest conviction signal.                                                                                     |
| **Annotated screenshot walkthrough** | Key screens with callouts. Lower risk, still concrete.                                                                                                         |
| **User journey flow**                | Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3 with outcomes at each stage. Shows the logic, not just the UI.                                                                        |
| **Metrics dashboard**                | Skip the walkthrough; show what the product produces — the outputs, the data, the ROI. Works for B2B tools where the buyer cares about outcomes, not features. |

#### Beat 6: Business Model

_How do you make money?_

| Option                         | Description                                                                                                            |
| ------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **SaaS tiers**                 | Free / Pro / Enterprise with clear upgrade triggers. The default for software.                                         |
| **Usage-based**                | Pay per API call, transaction, seat-hour. Aligns cost with value — attractive when usage scales with customer success. |
| **Marketplace take-rate**      | X% of GMV on every transaction. Works when you're the platform connecting supply and demand.                           |
| **Hybrid: license + services** | Platform fee plus implementation, training, or managed services. Common in enterprise and healthcare.                  |

#### Beat 7: Traction

_What evidence proves this is working?_

| Option                       | Description                                                                                                      |
| ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Revenue growth**           | MRR/ARR chart going up and to the right. The gold standard when you have it.                                     |
| **User growth + engagement** | DAU, retention curves, NPS. Works pre-revenue when users clearly love the product.                               |
| **Lighthouse customers**     | Name logos. "Company X, Company Y, and Company Z are paying customers." Three names are worth more than a chart. |
| **Waitlist / LOI / pilot**   | Pre-revenue evidence of demand. Letters of intent, signed pilots, waitlist depth. Works for pre-launch.          |

#### Beat 8: Team

_Why are you the ones to build this?_

| Option                      | Description                                                                                                              |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Founder-market fit**      | You lived this problem. You were the user, the operator, the researcher. The insight came from experience, not analysis. |
| **Technical moat via team** | Your CTO built X at Y. Your ML lead published the paper. The team has unfair technical advantage.                        |
| **Repeat founders**         | You've done this before. You built, scaled, and exited. The execution risk is lower because you've executed.             |
| **Complementary skills**    | Builder + seller + domain expert. The founding team covers product, growth, and industry knowledge with no gaps.         |

#### Beat 9: Ask

_What do you need and what will you do with it?_

| Option                        | Description                                                                                                                             |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Milestone-based ask**       | "We're raising $Xm to hit [3 specific milestones] in [timeframe]." Concrete, testable, accountable.                                     |
| **Hire-and-scale ask**        | "We're raising to hire [N roles] and scale [specific metric] from X to Y." Shows you know exactly where the bottleneck is.              |
| **Strategic partnership ask** | "Beyond capital, we're looking for [specific expertise, distribution, network]." Shows you've thought about what money alone can't buy. |

### Sequencing Notes

- The "Why Now" beat is the one most founders skip or phone in. It's the beat that separates "good idea" from "good idea whose time has come." Push hard on this.
- Product demo should feel like proof, not a tour. Show the hardest thing the product does, not the easiest.
- The Ask should tie back to the Market Size — the investor should feel the gap between where you are and what's possible.

## 2. MEDDPICC (Enterprise Sales Qualification)

The dominant enterprise sales framework. Each beat is a qualification dimension — the options represent different angles to surface the required information. Used to assess whether a deal is real and winnable.

**Best for:** $50K+ ACV enterprise deals, multi-stakeholder sales cycles

### Beats

#### Beat 1: Metrics

_What measurable business outcome does the buyer need?_

| Option              | Description                                                                                                                                           |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Revenue impact**  | "This will increase revenue by X% or $Y." The buyer's CFO cares. Requires: proof from similar customers, a model they can validate.                   |
| **Cost reduction**  | "This will save X hours per week / $Y per year." The efficiency play. Requires: current cost baseline, realistic adoption curve.                      |
| **Risk mitigation** | "This reduces the probability/cost of [specific bad outcome] by X%." The compliance/security play. Requires: quantified exposure, regulatory context. |
| **Speed-to-market** | "This cuts [process] from X weeks to Y days." The competitive advantage play. Requires: current timeline proof, bottleneck identification.            |

#### Beat 2: Economic Buyer

_Who has the budget authority to sign this deal?_

| Option                                | Description                                                                                                                                           |
| ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **C-suite sponsor identified**        | The CEO/CFO/CTO has been in a meeting and expressed interest. You know their name, their priorities, and their decision timeline.                     |
| **VP-level with delegated authority** | A VP has budget authority up to $X without C-suite sign-off. Faster cycle, lower ceiling.                                                             |
| **Committee buy**                     | No single signer — a procurement committee or board must approve. Longer cycle, need to map every stakeholder.                                        |
| **Champion-led discovery**            | Your internal champion believes they can get budget but hasn't confirmed the path. High risk — validate the budget path before investing more cycles. |

#### Beat 3: Decision Criteria

_What does the buyer need to see to say yes?_

| Option                                 | Description                                                                                                                            |
| -------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Technical requirements met**         | Specific integration points, security certifications, uptime SLAs. The buyer has a checklist — you need to see it and check every box. |
| **ROI threshold exceeded**             | The buyer has a payback period target (e.g., 12 months). You need to model to that number, not just promise value.                     |
| **Competitive differentiation proven** | The buyer is evaluating alternatives. You need to win on specific dimensions they've named — not the ones you'd choose.                |
| **Pilot success criteria**             | The buyer won't commit without a POC. Define success criteria upfront — what numbers constitute a "passed" pilot?                      |

#### Beat 4: Decision Process

_How does this organization actually make a buying decision?_

| Option                             | Description                                                                                                                               |
| ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Documented procurement flow**    | RFP → evaluation → shortlist → pilot → legal → sign. You have the timeline and the gatekeepers mapped.                                    |
| **Champion navigating internally** | Your champion is steering the process informally. Higher velocity, higher risk of surprise blockers.                                      |
| **Board approval required**        | Deals above $X go to the board. Adds 4–8 weeks and requires a board-ready summary. Plan for it.                                           |
| **Consensus culture**              | Multiple stakeholders need to agree; no single decision-maker. You need to identify and address each stakeholder's concerns individually. |

#### Beat 5: Identify Pain

_What is the specific, urgent pain driving this evaluation?_

| Option                     | Description                                                                                                                           |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Operational bottleneck** | A specific process is breaking under load. Teams are working around it manually. The pain is daily and visible.                       |
| **Compliance deadline**    | A regulation takes effect on [date]. Non-compliance has quantified consequences. The urgency is external and immovable.               |
| **Competitive threat**     | A competitor shipped something that's winning deals. The buyer is losing revenue now — not hypothetically.                            |
| **Executive mandate**      | A new CxO or board directive has made this a priority. The pain is political, not just operational. Map the mandate to your solution. |

#### Beat 6: Paper Process

_What's the legal and procurement path to a signed contract?_

| Option                        | Description                                                                                                           |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Standard MSA + Order Form** | The buyer uses a standard master agreement. Review cycles are predictable — 2–4 weeks for legal.                      |
| **Custom contract required**  | The buyer's legal team will redline everything. Budget 4–8 weeks and involve your legal early.                        |
| **Existing vendor vehicle**   | You can piggyback on an existing contract or approved vendor list. Fastest path — find out if it exists.              |
| **Procurement portal**        | The buyer uses Coupa, Ariba, or similar. There's a submission process with its own timeline — don't discover it late. |

#### Beat 7: Implicate the Pain

_What happens if the buyer does nothing?_

| Option                          | Description                                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Quantified cost of inaction** | "Every month you don't solve this costs $X in [lost revenue / excess headcount / compliance risk]." Make the status quo expensive. |
| **Competitive gap widens**      | "Your competitor already deployed this. Every quarter you wait, they compound their advantage." The pain of falling behind.        |
| **Regulatory exposure grows**   | "The enforcement date is [date]. After that, each incident costs $X in fines." The pain has a deadline.                            |
| **Team attrition risk**         | "Your best people are burning out on this manual process. You're one resignation away from a crisis." The pain is human.           |

#### Beat 8: Champion

_Who inside the organization is selling for you when you're not in the room?_

| Option                                              | Description                                                                                                                                |
| --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **True champion — influence + access + motivation** | They have the ear of the economic buyer, they've seen the problem firsthand, and solving it advances their career. All three must be true. |
| **Technical champion only**                         | They love the product but don't have political capital. Useful for technical validation, dangerous as your only internal advocate.         |
| **Executive sponsor**                               | A senior leader who wants this solved but won't do the internal legwork. You need a champion under them to execute.                        |
| **No champion identified**                          | You don't have someone who will sell for you internally. This is a red flag — either find one or deprioritize the deal.                    |

#### Beat 9: Competition

_Who else is the buyer evaluating?_

| Option                              | Description                                                                                                                                                   |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Named competitors in evaluation** | The buyer told you who else they're looking at. You know their strengths and weaknesses. Map your differentiation to the buyer's stated criteria — not yours. |
| **Status quo is the competitor**    | The real risk isn't another vendor — it's the buyer doing nothing or building in-house. Sell the cost of inaction.                                            |
| **Incumbent with switching costs**  | The buyer is on a competitor's platform and migration is painful. You need to prove the ROI exceeds the switching cost.                                       |
| **Open-source alternative**         | The buyer is considering a free option with internal engineering investment. Compete on total cost of ownership, not sticker price.                           |

### Sequencing Notes

- Metrics first, always. If you can't quantify the value, every subsequent beat is opinion.
- Champion is the single highest-signal beat. A deal without a true champion is a deal without a close date.
- Paper Process is the beat most reps discover too late. Surface it in the first two calls.

## 3. Challenger Sale

A persuasion framework where the seller teaches the buyer something they didn't know, reframes their worldview, then introduces the solution as the logical next step. Designed for complex sales where the buyer thinks they understand the problem but is wrong about the solution.

**Best for:** Consultative enterprise sales, category-creation pitches, displacing entrenched competitors

### Beats

#### Beat 1: The Warmer

_Build credibility by showing you understand their world._

| Option                                 | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| -------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Industry-specific data**             | "Companies in your space are spending X on Y, and the top quartile is doing Z differently." Show you've done homework. The buyer should think: "They know my business."                                    |
| **Peer comparison**                    | "We worked with [similar company] and they were facing the same three challenges you described." Social proof that doubles as empathy.                                                                     |
| **Acknowledge their current approach** | "Your team has built a solid [process/system] — we see a lot of companies at your stage doing exactly this." Validate before you challenge. The buyer must feel respected before they'll accept a reframe. |

#### Beat 2: The Reframe

_Teach the buyer something that challenges their assumption about the problem._

| Option                            | Description                                                                                                                                                                               |
| --------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **The hidden cost**               | "Most companies focus on [obvious metric]. But the real cost is [non-obvious metric] — and it's 3× larger." Redirect attention to where the pain actually lives.                          |
| **The wrong benchmark**           | "You're measuring success by [current KPI]. The companies pulling ahead are measuring [different KPI] — and it changes every decision downstream." Redefine what good looks like.         |
| **The structural flaw**           | "The approach you're using was designed for [old context]. The market has shifted to [new context] — and the old architecture can't follow." Challenge the foundation, not the execution. |
| **The counter-intuitive insight** | "The thing everyone in your industry believes — [common belief] — is actually wrong. Here's the data." Surprise creates openness. Only use this when the data is unambiguous.             |

#### Beat 3: Rational Drowning

_Quantify the cost of the current path until the buyer feels the weight._

| Option                     | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Compounding cost model** | "You're losing $X per month today. In 12 months, with current growth, that's $Y — and it compounds because [mechanism]." Make inaction expensive over time.                                                   |
| **Opportunity cost**       | "Every dollar/hour you spend on [current approach] is a dollar/hour not spent on [higher-value activity]. Here's what that gap looks like over 3 years." Reframe spending as missed opportunity.              |
| **Risk accumulation**      | "Each quarter you operate this way, your exposure to [specific risk] increases by X%. The probability of [bad event] crosses 50% by [date]." Make the timeline feel urgent through probability, not pressure. |

#### Beat 4: Emotional Impact

_Connect the rational case to what the buyer personally cares about._

| Option                          | Description                                                                                                                                                                                           |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Their team's pain**           | "Your people are spending X hours a week on [manual process]. That's not just a cost — it's why your best engineers are looking at other offers." Connect business metrics to human impact.           |
| **Their legacy**                | "You were hired to transform [function]. If this problem isn't solved in the next Y months, the transformation stalls — and that's what people will remember." Connect to career stakes.              |
| **Their customer's experience** | "Your end-users are experiencing [specific friction]. They don't blame the tool — they blame your company. That brand damage compounds silently." Connect internal operations to external perception. |

#### Beat 5: A New Way

_Present a different framework for solving the problem — not your product yet, just the approach._

| Option                             | Description                                                                                                                                                                               |
| ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Invert the workflow**            | "Instead of [current approach: do X then Y then Z], what if you [inverted approach: start with Z, automate X, eliminate Y]?" Show a structural rethink, not an incremental improvement.   |
| **Change the unit of work**        | "You're optimizing per-[current unit]. The companies winning this are optimizing per-[different unit]. That single change restructures the entire workflow." Shift the frame of analysis. |
| **Remove the bottleneck entirely** | "The bottleneck isn't slow [process] — it's having [process] at all. The new way eliminates the step, not speeds it up." Challenge whether the problem should exist.                      |

#### Beat 6: Your Solution

_Now — and only now — show how your product embodies the new way._

| Option                 | Description                                                                                                                                                      |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Architecture match** | "Our platform is built on the principle from Beat 5. Here's how [specific feature] implements [new way]." The product is the natural consequence of the reframe. |
| **Customer proof**     | "Company X adopted this approach 8 months ago. Here's what happened: [quantified result]." Let a peer's experience close the gap between theory and belief.      |
| **Live demonstration** | "Let me show you exactly what this looks like in practice." Transition from abstract framework to concrete product. The demo should feel inevitable, not salesy. |

### Sequencing Notes

- The Reframe is the most important beat. If the buyer doesn't accept the new framing, everything after it falls flat. Spend 60% of your prep time here.
- Rational Drowning must use the buyer's own numbers, not industry averages. Ask for their data in discovery; present it back to them in this beat.
- Never introduce your product before Beat 6. The temptation to solve early kills the reframe. Discipline here is the whole framework.

## 4. SPIN Selling

A question-driven discovery framework. Each beat is a type of question designed to guide the buyer from surface-level awareness to urgent, self-articulated need. Fewer beats, deeper per-beat branching.

**Best for:** Discovery calls, consultative selling, first meetings where you need to understand before you pitch

### Beats

#### Beat 1: Situation

_Understand the buyer's current state — facts, not opinions._

| Option               | Description                                                                                                                                       |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Process mapping**  | "Walk me through how your team handles [specific workflow] today — from trigger to completion." Get the literal steps. Don't assume.              |
| **Tool inventory**   | "What tools and systems does your team use for [function]? How do they connect?" Understand the stack before you pitch replacing part of it.      |
| **Team structure**   | "Who owns [function] today? How many people touch this process?" Map the human topology — it predicts adoption and resistance.                    |
| **Metrics baseline** | "How do you currently measure success for [function]? What does good look like today?" Establish the benchmark you'll later show is insufficient. |

#### Beat 2: Problem

_Surface the difficulties and dissatisfactions with the current state._

| Option                 | Description                                                                                                                                                               |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Friction probe**     | "What's the most frustrating part of that process for your team?" Let them name the pain. Their words are more powerful than yours.                                       |
| **Failure mode probe** | "When does this process break? What's the most common failure you see?" Identify where the system fails, not just where it's slow.                                        |
| **Workaround probe**   | "Are there things your team does to work around the limitations of [current tool/process]?" Workarounds are proof of unmet need — they're investing effort to compensate. |
| **Satisfaction probe** | "On a scale of 1–10, how satisfied is your team with [current approach]? What would make it a 10?" Quantify the gap between reality and desire.                           |

#### Beat 3: Implication

_Explore the consequences of the problem — make the buyer feel the downstream impact._

| Option                | Description                                                                                                                                                                     |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cascade impact**    | "When [problem] happens, what does that do to [downstream process]? And what does that mean for [business outcome]?" Chain the consequences until the buyer sees systemic risk. |
| **Cost implication**  | "How much time does your team spend dealing with [problem] per week/month? What would they be doing instead?" Convert frustration into dollars and lost opportunity.            |
| **Risk implication**  | "If [problem] happens during [critical moment — audit, launch, peak season], what's the exposure?" Make the problem scenario-specific and high-stakes.                          |
| **Scale implication** | "As your team grows from X to Y, how does this problem scale? Does it get proportionally worse or exponentially worse?" Show that the problem isn't static — it compounds.      |

#### Beat 4: Need-Payoff

_Get the buyer to articulate the value of solving the problem — in their own words._

| Option                    | Description                                                                                                                                                                          |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Value articulation**    | "If you could eliminate [problem], what would that mean for your team's output this quarter?" Let them describe the promised land. Their description carries more weight than yours. |
| **Priority confirmation** | "Where does solving this rank relative to your other priorities this half? What would change if it moved up?" Confirm this is worth acting on, not just worth discussing.            |
| **ROI self-calculation**  | "You mentioned [problem] costs X hours per week. If that went to zero, what would your team ship instead?" Guide them to calculate the payoff themselves.                            |
| **Decision trigger**      | "What would need to be true for you to solve this in the next [timeframe]?" Surface the conditions under which they'll act — then address them.                                      |

### Sequencing Notes

- SPIN is a discovery framework, not a pitch framework. The goal is to have the buyer convince themselves, not to convince the buyer.
- Situation questions should be minimized — do your homework before the call so you don't waste time on questions you could have answered from LinkedIn and their website.
- Implication questions are the hardest to ask well. Most reps skip from Problem to Need-Payoff. The gap between "I have a problem" and "I need to solve it now" is where Implication questions live.
- Never pitch during SPIN. If the buyer asks about your product, answer briefly and return to the question flow. The pitch comes after SPIN is complete.

## 5. Andy Raskin — 5-Beat (Original Short Form)

The compressed version of the framework already used in Throughline. Three fewer beats — designed for cold emails, 5-minute pitches, and elevator conversations.

**Best for:** Cold outreach, intro emails, lightning talks, tweet threads

### Beats

#### Beat 1: The River

_Name the undeniable shift._

| Option               | Description                                                                                                                                 |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Technology shift** | A specific technology crossed a threshold that changes what's possible. Name the technology, name the threshold, name what changed.         |
| **Market shift**     | Customer behavior, regulatory environment, or competitive landscape changed in a way that can't be reversed. The world moved — who noticed? |
| **Cultural shift**   | A belief or norm changed. What was once accepted is now questioned; what was once niche is now mainstream.                                  |

#### Beat 2: The Promised Land

_Paint the destination — identity-based, not feature-based._

| Option                        | Description                                                                                                                                             |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Identity transformation**   | "You become [new identity]." The buyer doesn't buy a tool — they buy who they become. Frame the destination as a role change, not a metric improvement. |
| **World transformation**      | "Your [team/company/market] looks like [desirable state]." Paint the macro picture — what does the landscape look like when this is solved?             |
| **Capability transformation** | "You can now do [previously impossible thing] as easily as [trivially simple thing]." Make the impossible feel inevitable.                              |

#### Beat 3: The Magic Wand

_What you bring that gets them there._

| Option                             | Description                                                                                                                                                  |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Single-sentence product thesis** | "We built [one thing] that [one outcome]." Maximum compression. If you need more than one sentence, you haven't found the core yet.                          |
| **Unification**                    | "Instead of [tool A + tool B + manual process C], we do [unified approach]." Show that your product isn't another tool — it's the replacement for the stack. |
| **Insight-driven**                 | "We realized [non-obvious insight]. So we built [product] around that principle." Lead with the idea, not the feature.                                       |

#### Beat 4: Proof

_Evidence that the treasure is real._

| Option                 | Description                                                                                                                   |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Customer name-drop** | "[Company X] went from [before state] to [after state] in [timeframe]." One specific, named example beats ten anonymous ones. |
| **Metric**             | "[Specific number] that proves the product works." Revenue, time saved, adoption rate — one number, made concrete.            |
| **Demo link**          | "See it yourself: [link]." Let the product speak. Works when the product is self-explanatory.                                 |

#### Beat 5: The Ask

_What you want from this interaction._

| Option              | Description                                                                                                                       |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Meeting request** | "Worth a 20-minute call this week?" Low commitment, high signal. The standard cold email close.                                   |
| **Intro request**   | "Who on your team owns [function]? Happy to reach out directly." Redirect to the right person if you're not talking to the buyer. |
| **Try-it request**  | "We have a free [trial/sandbox/pilot]. Want me to set one up?" Lower the activation energy. Works when the product sells itself.  |

### Sequencing Notes

- This is the version designed for written communication. Every word must earn its place.
- The River and Promised Land should together take less than 30 seconds to read aloud.
- Proof should be one sentence. If you need a paragraph of proof, you're pitching — switch to the 8-beat framework.
