# Domain-Specific Frameworks

Narrative structures tailored to specific industries. These frameworks embed domain knowledge — regulatory context, industry jargon, stakeholder dynamics — into the beat structure. They work because they speak the audience's language.

---

## 1. Clinical Trial Narrative

The standard structure for presenting a clinical-stage biotech or pharma investment thesis. Each beat maps to a section of a scientific advisory board presentation or investor update. The audience is scientifically literate but commercially minded.

**Best for:** Biotech investor pitches, clinical advisory board decks, partnership proposals, regulatory strategy briefs

### Beats

#### Beat 1: Unmet Need

_What disease or condition lacks adequate treatment? Quantify the human and economic cost._

| Option                        | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Epidemiological framing**   | "[X million] patients are diagnosed annually with [condition]. Current standard of care achieves [Y% response rate / Z-month survival / W quality-of-life score]. The gap between what patients need and what's available is [quantified]." Lead with the population size and the treatment gap. The audience thinks in patient populations.                        |
| **Patient journey framing**   | "A patient with [condition] today goes through: [diagnosis → treatment 1 → failure → treatment 2 → failure → limited options]. The average patient cycles through [N] treatments before [outcome]. Each cycle costs [time and money] and degrades [quality of life]." Walk the audience through what a real patient experiences. Numbers inform; journeys persuade. |
| **Economic burden framing**   | "[Condition] costs the healthcare system $[X]B annually: [$Y in direct treatment], [$Z in lost productivity], [$W in long-term care]. The economic case for a better treatment is [quantified ROI on investment]." Lead with money. Works for payer audiences and health economics-focused investors.                                                               |
| **Competitive treatment gap** | "Current treatments: [Drug A] achieves [efficacy] with [side effects]. [Drug B] achieves [efficacy] with [side effects]. No approved treatment addresses [specific dimension: durability, combination, biomarker-selected population, etc.]." Map the competitive landscape to show exactly where the gap is. The audience should see the white space.              |

#### Beat 2: Mechanism of Action

_How does your therapeutic approach work? Why should it succeed where others haven't?_

| Option                       | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
| ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Target biology**           | "The [target/pathway] is [validated / emerging] in [condition]. Evidence: [key publication 1], [key publication 2]. Our approach [agonizes / inhibits / modulates] [target] via [mechanism], which addresses the disease at [point in the pathological cascade]." Connect the biology to the disease. The audience needs to believe the target is right before they'll believe the drug is right.                     |
| **Differentiated mechanism** | "Existing therapies target [X]. We target [Y], which sits [upstream / downstream / orthogonally]. This matters because [specific reason targeting Y is better: broader efficacy, fewer resistance pathways, combination potential]." Show what's different about your approach — not just that it's new, but why the difference matters biologically.                                                                 |
| **Preclinical evidence**     | "In [model type] models: [efficacy data]. In [second model]: [efficacy data]. Toxicology: [safety findings]. The preclinical package supports [specific claim about likely clinical performance]." Let the data make the case. Be precise about what the data shows and doesn't show.                                                                                                                                 |
| **Platform advantage**       | "Our [platform: antibody-drug conjugate / gene therapy vector / mRNA delivery / small molecule library] enables [specific advantage: tissue selectivity, redosability, manufacturing scalability]. This is not a one-drug story — the platform generates [pipeline breadth]." When the technology is the moat, lead with the platform. The individual drug is the proof point; the platform is the investment thesis. |

#### Beat 3: Clinical Evidence

_What do the data show? Be precise, honest, and contextualized._

| Option                       | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Headline efficacy**        | "In our Phase [X] trial ([trial name], N=[enrollment]): [primary endpoint] = [result] (p=[value]). [Key secondary endpoint] = [result]. For context, the current standard of care achieves [benchmark] on the same endpoint." Lead with the number that matters most. Contextualize against the standard of care — the audience will do this comparison anyway. |
| **Safety and tolerability**  | "In [N] patients treated to date: [most common AEs with rates]. [Serious AEs]: [count and description]. No [specific feared toxicity] observed. The therapeutic window is [assessment]." Safety data builds confidence. Be forthright about adverse events — the audience will find them anyway, and transparency builds trust.                                 |
| **Subgroup signal**          | "In the [biomarker-defined / stage-defined / prior-treatment-defined] subgroup (n=[subset]): [efficacy metric] = [value], compared to [value] in the overall population. This suggests [hypothesis about patient selection]." Subgroup data can transform a modest overall result into a compelling precision medicine story. Flag it when the signal is there. |
| **Durability and follow-up** | "Median follow-up: [months]. [Durability metric]: [value]. [X%] of responders remain in response at [timepoint]. The durability data are [mature / immature] — updated data expected [date]." For oncology and chronic diseases, durability often matters more than initial response rate.                                                                      |

#### Beat 4: Trial Design

_How is the pivotal study designed? Does the design de-risk the program?_

| Option                   | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Registrational path**  | "Pivotal trial: Phase [X], [design: randomized / single-arm / adaptive], N=[target enrollment], [comparator arm if applicable]. Primary endpoint: [endpoint]. Expected readout: [date]. Regulatory pathway: [standard approval / accelerated / breakthrough]." The audience needs to see the path from here to approval. Make it concrete.                                                                                                           |
| **Adaptive design**      | "The trial uses [adaptive element: interim analysis with futility/success boundaries, dose optimization, biomarker enrichment]. This reduces [specific risk: running a full trial on a losing dose, enrolling patients unlikely to respond]." Adaptive designs show sophistication and risk management. Explain what the adaptation protects against.                                                                                                |
| **Combination strategy** | "The trial tests [drug] in combination with [standard of care / partner drug]. Rationale: [biological rationale for combination]. The control arm is [description]. The combination addresses [limitation of monotherapy]." Many programs are combination stories. Be explicit about why the combination is needed and what the monotherapy can't do alone.                                                                                          |
| **Biomarker strategy**   | "Enrollment is [biomarker-selected / all-comers with prospective biomarker collection]. The biomarker is [description], prevalence [X%] in [population]. If the biomarker-selected population confirms, the commercial opportunity is [quantified] with a higher probability of success." Biomarker selection reduces trial risk by enriching for responders. Quantify the trade-off: smaller addressable population, higher probability of success. |

#### Beat 5: Regulatory Path

_What's the path to approval? What's the timeline and risk?_

| Option                        | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Breakthrough / fast track** | "We have [Breakthrough Therapy / Fast Track / Orphan Drug] designation. This provides: [specific benefits: rolling review, more frequent FDA meetings, priority review voucher]. Expected NDA/BLA submission: [date]. Expected approval: [date]." Designations signal FDA alignment and accelerate timelines. Name the specific benefits.                |
| **Standard pathway**          | "We expect to file [NDA/BLA/MAA] in [date] based on [pivotal data readout]. Standard review timeline: [X months]. We have had [N] pre-submission meetings with FDA. Key discussion points resolved: [list]. Open questions: [list]." For standard pathways, the audience wants to know you've been talking to the regulators and there are no surprises. |
| **Global strategy**           | "US filing: [date]. EU filing: [date]. Japan filing: [date]. The regulatory strategy is [harmonized / sequential]. Key differences by region: [specific regulatory requirements that differ]." For large-market drugs, show the global path. Different regulators have different requirements — show you know what they are.                             |
| **Risk factors**              | "Key regulatory risks: [risk 1: endpoint acceptance], [risk 2: safety signal], [risk 3: manufacturing]. Mitigation: [for each risk, specific mitigation strategy]." Name the risks before the audience asks. Your credibility increases when you acknowledge what could go wrong and show you've planned for it.                                         |

#### Beat 6: Commercial Opportunity

_If approved, how big is the market and how will you capture it?_

| Option                        | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Peak sales estimate**       | "Addressable patient population: [N]. Expected market share: [X%] by year [Y]. Peak net revenue estimate: $[Z]. Key assumptions: [price, uptake curve, competitive entries]." The bottom-up model. Name every assumption so the audience can stress-test it themselves.                                                           |
| **Pricing and reimbursement** | "Expected price: $[X] per [course / year / dose]. Comparable pricing: [drug A] at $[Y], [drug B] at $[Z]. Payer willingness: [assessment based on clinical differentiation and health economic data]." Pricing is where clinical value meets commercial reality. Show that your price is defensible given the clinical data.      |
| **Competitive dynamics**      | "Expected competitive entries in the next [N] years: [competitor 1] with [approach, timeline], [competitor 2] with [approach, timeline]. Our differentiation: [specific advantages that survive competitive entry]." No drug launches in a vacuum. Show you've modeled the competitive landscape and your advantages are durable. |
| **Launch strategy**           | "Target prescribers: [specialty, count]. Key opinion leaders engaged: [count]. Medical affairs activities: [list]. Expected launch timeline: [date]. Ramp assumptions: [month-by-month projection for year 1]." The audience wants to know you can sell it, not just make it. Show commercial readiness.                          |

#### Beat 7: Investment Thesis

_Why now? Why us? What's the catalyst?_

| Option                  | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Near-term catalyst**  | "The next value-creating event is [data readout / regulatory decision / partnership announcement] expected in [timeframe]. The current valuation [does / does not] reflect this catalyst." The audience buys catalysts, not stories. Name the next event and its expected impact.                    |
| **Platform value**      | "Beyond [lead program], the platform enables: [pipeline program 2, 3, 4]. Each represents [addressable market]. The lead program de-risks the platform; the platform justifies the valuation." For platform companies, the lead program is proof of concept. The value is in the pipeline behind it. |
| **Partnering leverage** | "We are positioned for [partnership / out-licensing / co-development] for [specific programs or geographies]. The data package supports: [deal structure]. Comparable transactions: [deal 1], [deal 2]." Many biotech investments are won or lost on the exit thesis. Show the path to a deal.       |
| **Team and execution**  | "The team has [N] prior approvals. Key hires in the last [timeframe]: [name, role, background]. The organization is built to execute [current stage] and transition to [next stage]." For early-stage companies, the team IS the investment thesis. Show they've done this before.                   |

### Sequencing Notes

- Unmet need MUST come first. If the audience doesn't believe the need is real and large, nothing after it matters.
- Clinical evidence should be presented in context — never show your data without showing the standard of care comparator. The audience evaluates relative improvement, not absolute numbers.
- Commercial opportunity should feel conservative. Investors discount aggressive projections. A conservative estimate they believe is worth more than an aggressive one they don't.

## 2. Payer Value Story

The narrative structure for convincing insurance companies, PBMs, and government payers to cover and reimburse a therapy. The audience cares about population-level outcomes and budget impact, not individual patient stories.

**Best for:** Payer presentations, AMCP dossiers, health technology assessment submissions, formulary committee decks, value-based contracting proposals

### Beats

#### Beat 1: Disease Burden

_How much does this condition cost the health system? Population-level, not individual._

| Option                               | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             |
| ------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Direct cost burden**               | "[Condition] costs the US healthcare system $[X]B annually. Breakdown: hospitalizations ($[A]), outpatient visits ($[B]), pharmacy ($[C]), long-term care ($[D]). Source: [claims analysis / published study]." Lead with the system-level cost. Payers think in budgets, not patients.                                                                                                 |
| **Indirect cost burden**             | "Beyond direct medical costs: $[X]B in lost productivity, $[Y]B in caregiver burden, $[Z]B in disability payments. Total societal burden: $[W]B. [Condition] ranks [Nth] among chronic diseases in economic impact." Include indirect costs when they're large and when the payer's incentives extend beyond direct medical spend (e.g., employer-sponsored plans, Medicaid).           |
| **Trend projection**                 | "Disease prevalence is [growing at X% annually / stable / declining]. At current trajectory, the cost burden reaches $[Y]B by [year]. The primary driver is [aging population / diagnostic improvements / lifestyle factors]." Show the payer that the problem isn't static — it's getting worse. The investment case for a better treatment strengthens as the alternative cost grows. |
| **Subpopulation cost concentration** | "[X%] of patients account for [Y%] of total costs. These high-cost patients are characterized by [clinical profile]. An intervention targeting this subpopulation has outsized budget impact." Payers love interventions that address the most expensive patients. Show them the leverage point.                                                                                        |

#### Beat 2: Current Standard of Care

_What treatments are covered today? How well do they work? What do they cost?_

| Option                             | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Treatment pathway map**          | "Current pathway: 1L [treatment, cost, outcomes]. 2L [treatment, cost, outcomes]. 3L+ [treatment, cost, outcomes]. Total cost per patient over [timeframe]: $[X]." Map the full treatment journey with costs at each line. The payer needs to see the total cost of the current approach — your drug replaces part of this spending.            |
| **Outcomes gap**                   | "Current SOC achieves: [efficacy metric] = [value]. [Quality of life metric] = [value]. [Hospitalization rate] = [value]. The gap between current outcomes and guideline targets is [quantified]." Show that the current standard falls short of clinical guidelines. The payer is under-treating the disease with current options.             |
| **Generic / biosimilar landscape** | "Current generic options: [list with costs]. Branded options: [list with costs]. Generic utilization: [X%]. The current cost floor is $[Y] per patient per year." If cheap options exist, you need to show why they're insufficient. If they don't, you have more pricing latitude.                                                             |
| **Treatment failure rate**         | "[X%] of patients fail [current SOC] within [timeframe]. Failure costs: $[Y] per patient (hospitalization, next-line therapy, complications). The total cost of treatment failure in [condition] is $[Z]B annually." Treatment failure is where the budget bleeds. If your drug reduces failure, you're saving the payer money on the back end. |

#### Beat 3: Clinical Differentiation

_How is your therapy better? Speak in endpoints the payer cares about._

| Option                                   | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Superiority on primary endpoint**      | "In [trial name]: [your drug] achieved [endpoint] = [value] vs. [comparator] = [value]. Difference: [absolute and relative]. NNT: [number needed to treat]. This represents a [X%] improvement over current SOC." Payers want the head-to-head comparison. If you have randomized comparator data, lead with it. NNT translates efficacy into population impact. |
| **Reduced hospitalizations / ER visits** | "[Your drug] reduced [hospitalizations / ER visits] by [X%] compared to [comparator]. At $[Y] per hospitalization, this translates to $[Z] in avoided inpatient costs per patient per year." This is the payer's favorite endpoint. Hospitalization avoidance is the clearest budget impact signal.                                                              |
| **Quality of life improvement**          | "[Your drug] improved [QoL metric: EQ-5D, SF-36, disease-specific PRO] by [value] vs. [comparator]. This improvement exceeds the minimum clinically important difference (MCID) of [threshold]." QoL data supports the story but rarely drives the decision alone. Pair it with cost or hospitalization data.                                                    |
| **Safety advantage**                     | "[Your drug] had [lower rate of specific AE] compared to [comparator]: [X%] vs. [Y%]. The cost of managing [AE] is $[Z] per event. The safety advantage translates to $[W] in avoided AE management costs per 100 treated patients." Safety advantages have direct cost implications. Quantify them.                                                             |

#### Beat 4: Economic Value

_What's the cost-effectiveness? What's the budget impact?_

| Option                            | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cost-effectiveness analysis**   | "ICER: $[X] per QALY gained. This is [below / within / above] the commonly cited willingness-to-pay threshold of $[50K–150K] per QALY. Sensitivity analysis: ICER remains below threshold under [scenarios]." The ICER is the payer's primary decision metric. If you're below threshold, lead with it. If you're above, lead with a different framing. |
| **Budget impact model**           | "For a plan with [N] eligible members: Year 1 budget impact: +$[X] (pharmacy) / -$[Y] (medical). Net budget impact: $[Z]. By Year 3: net savings of $[W] per year." Show the trajectory. Most new drugs increase pharmacy spend but decrease medical spend. The payer needs to see when the crossover happens.                                          |
| **Cost per responder**            | "Cost per responder: $[X] with [your drug] vs. $[Y] with [comparator]. [Your drug] costs [more/less] per patient who actually benefits." When the overall ICER is unfavorable but the drug works well in a defined subgroup, cost-per-responder reframes the value.                                                                                     |
| **Total cost of care comparison** | "Total cost of care over [timeframe]: $[X] with [your drug] (pharmacy: $[A], medical: $[B], AE management: $[C]) vs. $[Y] with [comparator] (pharmacy: $[D], medical: $[E], AE management: $[F])." The most comprehensive view. Show all the cost buckets, not just the pharmacy line.                                                                  |

#### Beat 5: Real-World Evidence

_Beyond clinical trials: what does the evidence show in practice?_

| Option                        | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Claims data analysis**      | "In a retrospective analysis of [N] patients in [database]: [outcome metric] = [value]. Consistent with [pivotal trial results / better than pivotal trial results]. Key difference from trial: [real-world population characteristic]." Claims data shows payers what happens when the drug is used outside controlled conditions.                |
| **Registry data**             | "The [disease] registry ([registry name], N=[enrollment]) shows: [outcome data for patients treated with your drug]. Median follow-up: [months]. Persistence rate at 12 months: [X%]." Registries provide long-term, real-world outcomes data. Persistence data is especially important — a drug that patients stop taking doesn't generate value. |
| **Comparative effectiveness** | "In [real-world study comparing your drug to alternatives]: [your drug] was associated with [X% lower hospitalization / Y% better persistence / Z% lower total cost]. Adjusted for [confounders]." Real-world comparative effectiveness directly addresses the payer's question: "Is this better than what I'm already covering?"                  |
| **Adherence and persistence** | "Real-world adherence: [X%] at 6 months, [Y%] at 12 months. Compared to [comparator class]: [higher/lower] by [Z percentage points]. Clinical impact of adherence difference: [quantified]." Adherence is the payer's hidden variable. A more effective drug that patients don't take is worse than a less effective drug they do.                 |

#### Beat 6: Access Proposal

_What are you asking for? Formulary tier, restrictions, contracting terms._

| Option                                       | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Unrestricted access**                      | "We recommend unrestricted formulary placement at Tier [X]. WAC: $[price]. Net price after rebate: $[price]. The clinical evidence supports broad access for [indicated population]." The strongest ask. Only appropriate when the clinical and economic case is overwhelming.                                                                                                       |
| **Step therapy position**                    | "We recommend placement after [specific prior therapy] failure. This is consistent with [clinical guideline] and ensures the drug reaches patients who need it while managing utilization." Concede a step edit to get on formulary. Shows the payer you're realistic about positioning.                                                                                             |
| **Value-based contract**                     | "We propose [outcomes-based / indication-based / volume-based] contracting: [specific terms]. If [outcome metric] is not achieved, [rebate structure]. This aligns our price with our clinical promise." Value-based contracts reduce payer risk. They signal confidence in the drug's real-world performance.                                                                       |
| **Restricted access with clinical criteria** | "We recommend coverage with prior authorization requiring: [criterion 1: diagnosis confirmation], [criterion 2: prior treatment failure], [criterion 3: prescriber specialty]. These criteria ensure appropriate use and are consistent with the pivotal trial population." Restrictions that mirror the clinical trial population are defensible and reduce off-label use concerns. |

### Sequencing Notes

- Disease burden sets the ceiling. If the condition costs the system $100M, the payer won't pay for a $200M solution. Establish the economic scale first.
- Never present clinical data without economic context. The payer's question isn't "does it work?" — it's "does it work well enough to justify the cost relative to alternatives?"
- The access proposal should feel like a natural conclusion, not a negotiation opening. If the preceding beats were convincing, the ask should seem reasonable.

## 3. SaaS Investor Narrative

The standard structure for B2B SaaS companies raising capital. Overlaps with the Sequoia framework but emphasizes metrics unique to the SaaS business model. The audience thinks in terms of ARR, NRR, CAC, and LTV.

**Best for:** SaaS Series A–C fundraising, board presentations, investor updates

### Beats

#### Beat 1: Category and Timing

_What category are you in — or creating — and why is now the moment?_

| Option                                  | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| --------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Existing category, better execution** | "The [category] market is $[X]B and growing [Y%] annually. Current leaders ([names]) have [specific weakness]. We win by [specific differentiation]." You're in a known market and doing it better. Lower risk for investors, but you need a clear reason you win.                                                                                  |
| **Category creation**                   | "There is no existing category for what we do. The closest analogues — [existing categories] — miss [specific dimension]. We're defining [new category name]: [definition]. The market is [estimated size based on analogous spending]." Higher risk, higher reward. The investor needs to believe the category will exist and that you'll lead it. |
| **Platform shift**                      | "The shift from [old paradigm] to [new paradigm] is [X%] complete. Companies that rode [previous shift] became [$B companies]. We're the [equivalent position] in [new shift]." Pattern-match to a previous platform shift the investor already believes in. AI, cloud, mobile — each created a generation of category leaders.                     |

#### Beat 2: ICP and Wedge

_Who do you sell to and how do you get in the door?_

| Option                  | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Specific persona**    | "Our ICP: [title] at [company size/type] who is responsible for [function] and currently struggling with [specific problem]. They find us through [channel]. The typical buying cycle is [length]." Be so specific the investor can picture the person. "VP of Engineering at 200–1,000 employee SaaS companies" is better than "engineering leaders." |
| **Bottom-up adoption**  | "Individual [role] users start using the product for free. When [N] users at one company are active, the account converts to paid. Current free → paid conversion rate: [X%]. Average time to conversion: [Y months]." The PLG model. The wedge is the individual user; the sale is to the company.                                                    |
| **Top-down enterprise** | "We sell to [buyer title] via [sales motion: outbound / partner / event]. Average deal size: $[X]k. Sales cycle: [Y months]. Win rate: [Z%]." The enterprise model. The investor needs to believe the sales motion is repeatable.                                                                                                                      |
| **Land and expand**     | "We enter with [specific use case, low ACV] and expand into [adjacent use cases] as the customer matures. Average initial deal: $[X]k. Average account at 24 months: $[Y]k. Expansion rate: [Z%]." The wedge is small; the account grows. This model requires strong NRR to work.                                                                      |

#### Beat 3: Product and Moat

_What does the product do and why is it hard to replicate?_

| Option                        | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Data moat**                 | "As customers use the product, we accumulate [type of data]. This data enables [specific capability] that improves with scale. Competitors starting today would need [X months/years] of [data type] to match our [capability]." Data moats compound. Show the flywheel.                                                                  |
| **Network effects**           | "Each new [user/customer/integration] makes the product more valuable for every other [user/customer/integration]. Current network size: [N]. Value per node increases at [rate]." Network effects are the strongest moat. But they must be specific — not "more users = better" but "more [specific thing] = [specific improvement]."    |
| **Workflow integration**      | "The product sits in the [critical workflow] between [tool A] and [tool B]. Once integrated, switching cost is [high/moderate] because [specific reason: data migration, retraining, downstream dependencies]." Integration depth creates switching costs. The more deeply embedded in the customer's workflow, the stickier the product. |
| **Technical differentiation** | "Our [architecture / algorithm / approach] enables [specific capability] that [competitor approach] structurally cannot achieve. The technical advantage: [description]. Time to replicate: [estimate]." Technical moats are real but often temporary. Show awareness of how long the advantage lasts.                                    |

#### Beat 4: Metrics

_Show the numbers that prove the business works._

| Option                 | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Growth metrics**     | "ARR: $[X]M. YoY growth: [Y%]. MoM growth (last 6 months): [Z%]. Net new ARR last quarter: $[W]k." Growth is the headline. Show the trend, not just the current number.                                                                              |
| **Retention metrics**  | "Gross retention: [X%]. Net retention (NRR): [Y%]. Logo churn: [Z%] monthly. Cohort analysis: [description of how retention changes with age]." NRR above 120% means the business grows even without new customers. Below 100% means you're leaking. |
| **Efficiency metrics** | "CAC: $[X]. Payback period: [Y months]. LTV/CAC: [Z]. Burn multiple: [W]." Efficiency tells the investor how much money you need to keep growing. A burn multiple below 2× is strong; above 3× is concerning.                                        |
| **Engagement metrics** | "DAU/MAU: [X%]. Average sessions per user per week: [Y]. Feature adoption: [Z%] of users use [core feature] within first [timeframe]." Engagement predicts retention. Strong engagement today means strong retention tomorrow.                       |

#### Beat 5: Go-to-Market

_How do you acquire and grow customers? Is it repeatable?_

| Option                   | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Product-led growth**   | "Free tier → self-serve paid → enterprise. Conversion funnel: [registration] → [activation: X%] → [paid: Y%] → [expansion: Z%]. Blended CAC: $[W] (organic: $[A], paid: $[B])." PLG economics. The investor wants to see that organic acquisition is a meaningful percentage — pure paid acquisition isn't PLG.                            |
| **Sales-led growth**     | "Sales team: [N AEs, M SDRs]. Quota per AE: $[X]k. Attainment: [Y%]. Pipeline coverage: [Z]×. Ramping AEs: [count, expected quota month]." Sales efficiency. The investor wants to see a machine that works: predictable pipeline, reasonable quota, consistent attainment.                                                                |
| **Partner-led growth**   | "[X%] of new ARR comes through [partner type: agency, technology, reseller]. Key partners: [names]. Partner economics: [revenue share / referral fee]. Scaling plan: [N new partners in next Y months]." Partner channels are leveraged growth. Show that partners are motivated (economics) and productive (ARR contribution).            |
| **Community-led growth** | "Community size: [N]. Active contributors: [M]. Community → trial conversion: [X%]. Community-sourced content / templates / plugins: [Y]. Community is our [top/second] acquisition channel." Community creates belonging and switching costs simultaneously. Show the community is genuinely active, not just a Slack channel with a bot. |

#### Beat 6: Team and Culture

_Who's building this and how do they work?_

| Option                  | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Founder-market fit**  | "The founding team spent [N years] in [domain] before starting [company]. [Founder 1] built [relevant thing] at [relevant company]. [Founder 2] scaled [relevant metric] at [relevant company]." The team's credibility is domain-specific. Generic "smart people" isn't enough — show why these specific people are the right ones for this specific problem. |
| **Engineering culture** | "Engineering team: [N]. Ship velocity: [deployments per week / PRs merged per week]. Technical debt approach: [description]. Key architectural decisions: [list]." For technical investors, the engineering culture is a leading indicator of product velocity.                                                                                                |
| **Key hires**           | "Recent key hires: [Name, role, previously at Company]. [Name, role, previously at Company]. These hires signal [what capability we're building: enterprise sales, platform engineering, international expansion]." Hiring is strategy. The roles you're filling tell the investor where you're investing.                                                     |
| **Org structure**       | "Team: [N total]. Breakdown: [engineering: X, product: Y, sales: Z, CS: W, G&A: V]. Ratio targets: [what ratios you're optimizing for and why]." The org chart is a budget allocation. Show you're investing in the right functions for your stage.                                                                                                            |

#### Beat 7: The Ask and Use of Funds

_How much, for what, and what milestones will it unlock?_

| Option              | Description                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Milestone-based** | "Raising $[X]M at $[Y]M pre-money. Use of funds: [A% engineering: specific hires], [B% sales: specific hires and quota capacity], [C% marketing: specific programs]. Milestones: hit $[Z]M ARR by [date], reach [W] customers, launch [feature]." Tie every dollar to a milestone. The investor should be able to track whether you hit the plan.                    |
| **Runway-based**    | "Raising $[X]M for [Y months] of runway. Current burn: $[Z]k/month. Expected burn at full deployment: $[W]k/month. This runway gets us to [profitability / next raise at materially different valuation / key milestone]." When the milestone is less certain, frame it as runway. Show that the raise lasts long enough to prove the next thing.                    |
| **Strategic round** | "Beyond capital, we're looking for: [specific strategic value: distribution partnership, industry expertise, follow-on capacity, board member with relevant background]. Our ideal lead investor has [specific characteristics]." Signal that you're choosing investors, not just taking money. Works when you have leverage (multiple term sheets, strong metrics). |

### Sequencing Notes

- Metrics (Beat 4) is where most SaaS pitches are won or lost. Strong metrics can compensate for a weak narrative; weak metrics cannot be compensated by a strong narrative.
- The moat question (Beat 3) gets harder as the company grows. Early-stage moats are often "we're further along." Growth-stage moats must be structural. Have an honest answer for your stage.
- Use of funds should imply the next round. If the investor gives you $10M and you hit the milestones, what does the Series [N+1] story look like? Paint that picture without saying it explicitly.
